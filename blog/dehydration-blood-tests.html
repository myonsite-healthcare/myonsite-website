Which Blood Tests Indicate Dehydration? A Lab Science Guide

Lab Science 📅 Feb 1, 2026 ⏱️ 5 min read ✍️ myOnsite Healthcare Team
Home > Blog > Lab Science

Key Takeaways

  • Elevated BUN (blood urea nitrogen) and creatinine ratios are primary dehydration markers
  • High serum sodium (hypernatremia) and elevated hematocrit indicate fluid loss and hemoconcentration
  • Uric acid elevation can signal chronic dehydration in vulnerable populations
  • Proper hydration before blood work improves accuracy and prevents misdiagnosis

Your Blood Tells the Story of Your Hydration

When you're dehydrated, your blood composition changes. Water loss concentrates solutes—minerals, proteins, blood cells—in your bloodstream. These changes show up in routine blood work, creating a tell-tale signature that reveals dehydration. Let's explore what these markers mean and how they affect your health and lab results.

The Primary Dehydration Markers

1. Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN) Elevation

BUN measures urea, a nitrogen-containing waste product from protein metabolism that your kidneys filter out. Normal range: 7-20 mg/dL.

When you're dehydrated, blood volume decreases. Your kidneys receive less blood flow, so they filter and reabsorb more urea back into circulation. Result: BUN rises. A BUN >20 mg/dL often signals dehydration, particularly if other markers align.

Clinical significance: BUN is one of the first markers to rise with dehydration. If your BUN is high but your creatinine is normal, dehydration is likely. If both are high, kidney disease might be the culprit.

2. Elevated BUN-to-Creatinine Ratio

This ratio is more specific than BUN alone. Creatinine, another kidney waste product, also increases with dehydration, but less dramatically than BUN. Normal BUN:Creatinine ratio is 10:1 to 20:1.

In dehydration, this ratio expands—BUN rises much faster than creatinine, creating a ratio of 25:1 or higher. This disproportionate elevation is a classic dehydration signature.

Example: A patient has BUN of 28 mg/dL and creatinine of 0.9 mg/dL. Ratio: 31:1. This strongly suggests dehydration, not kidney disease.

3. Serum Sodium (Hypernatremia)

Sodium is crucial for nerve and muscle function. Normal range: 136-145 mEq/L. Dehydration concentrates sodium in the blood, pushing levels above 145 mEq/L.

Hypernatremia signals net water loss relative to sodium. This is common in elderly patients, athletes in extreme heat, or patients with diarrhea or vomiting.

Symptoms of hypernatremia: thirst, weakness, confusion, irritability, and seizures in severe cases. If your sodium is elevated, drink water—don't add salt.

4. Elevated Hematocrit (Hemoconcentration)

Hematocrit measures the percentage of red blood cells in blood. Normal: 38-50% for men, 35-45% for women.

With dehydration, water loss concentrates red blood cells. Hematocrit rises above normal, a phenomenon called hemoconcentration. This is one reason dehydrated athletes sometimes show falsely elevated hemoglobin levels.

Clinical pearl: If both your hemoglobin and hematocrit are elevated, dehydration is likely. If only hemoglobin is high but hematocrit is normal, anemia or polycythemia might be the cause.

Secondary Dehydration Markers

Uric Acid Elevation

Uric acid, a byproduct of purine metabolism, is normally 3.5-7.2 mg/dL. Dehydration concentrates uric acid in the blood, raising levels and increasing gout risk. This is particularly common in:

  • Elderly patients in warm climates
  • Athletes during intense training
  • Patients with diuretic use (water pills)
  • People with restricted fluid intake

Albumin Elevation

Albumin, the most abundant blood protein, is normally 3.5-5.0 g/dL. With dehydration, albumin concentrates, rising above normal. This is less specific than BUN but contributes to the dehydration picture when multiple markers align.

Hydrate Before Your Blood Draw

Proper hydration improves blood draw accuracy and ensures your results reflect your true health status, not dehydration artifacts. Drink water the day before and the morning of your blood draw.

Chronic vs. Acute Dehydration

Dehydration patterns differ based on speed and duration:

Acute Dehydration

Rapid fluid loss over hours to days. Causes: vomiting, diarrhea, excessive sweating, heat exposure. Laboratory changes are dramatic and reversible with rehydration.

Chronic Dehydration

Gradual, ongoing fluid loss over weeks to months. Causes: inadequate water intake (common in elderly patients), persistent diarrhea, diuretic medications. Lab changes are subtle but consistent, and the body makes adaptations (increased ADH production, aldosterone activation).

Clinical relevance: Elderly patients are particularly vulnerable to chronic dehydration because their thirst mechanism weakens with age. Regular blood work helps detect this.

Who Is Most at Risk for Dehydration?

  • Elderly patients: Diminished thirst sensation, medications (diuretics), mobility limitations limiting water access
  • Athletes and outdoor workers: Excessive sweating, heat exposure, insufficient fluid replacement
  • Patients with diarrhea or vomiting: Acute fluid loss from gastroenteritis, medications, or medical conditions
  • Patients on diuretics: Blood pressure or heart failure medications that increase urinary output
  • Diabetic patients: High blood sugar draws water into the urine (osmotic diuresis)

How Proper Hydration Affects Blood Draw Accuracy

Blood composition is sensitive to hydration status. A dehydrated patient's labs can misrepresent their true health:

Potential Misdiagnoses from Dehydration

  • False anemia: Elevated hematocrit from hemoconcentration mimics polycythemia; can mask concurrent anemia
  • Apparent kidney disease: Elevated BUN and creatinine suggest kidney problems, when actually the kidneys are working harder due to low blood volume
  • Electrolyte imbalances: Hypernatremia from dehydration may prompt unnecessary sodium restriction
  • False gout diagnosis: Elevated uric acid misattributed to gout when hydration is the culprit

Best Practices for Accurate Labs

  • Drink 8-10 glasses of water the day before your draw
  • Drink 1-2 glasses of water the morning of your draw (unless fasting is required)
  • Avoid excess caffeine and alcohol the day before—they increase urination
  • Eat normally unless instructed to fast
  • Get adequate sleep the night before
  • Avoid strenuous exercise the day before (can elevate BUN and creatinine)

"Dehydration is silent but detectable. Your blood work can reveal it, and treating it prevents complications from kidney stress to gout attacks."

When to Seek Medical Attention

Severe dehydration requires immediate care:

  • Severe dizziness or fainting
  • Confusion or altered mental status
  • Rapid heartbeat (over 120 bpm at rest)
  • No urination for 8+ hours
  • Dark urine (indicates concentration)
  • Inability to keep fluids down (persistent vomiting)

The Bottom Line on Dehydration and Blood Tests

Your blood tells the story of your hydration. Elevated BUN, high BUN:creatinine ratios, hypernatremia, and hemoconcentration are all markers of dehydration that appear in routine labs. By understanding these markers, you can:

  • Recognize dehydration's early warning signs
  • Ensure your blood work accurately reflects your health (not dehydration artifacts)
  • Protect yourself from misdiagnosis
  • Make informed decisions about hydration and preventive health

Before your next blood draw, hydrate well. It's simple, free, and ensures the most accurate results.
